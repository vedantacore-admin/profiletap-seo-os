## India-specific angle
The NFC business card market in India has a specific adoption constraint no existing page addresses: NFC hardware is universal on Indian Android devices (95% of the market) but the behaviour pattern — tapping rather than scanning — is less established than QR scanning, which UPI normalised for the whole country. The winning India-market NFC card page explains both the NFC opportunity AND why QR backup matters specifically for Indian users, mentions that WhatsApp follow-up means the card profile must mask personal contact details, and names the actual India-native competitors (TapMo, TapOnn, ProfileTap) rather than positioning against global tools that most Indian buyers have never encountered.

## Notes for writer
- Open the page by acknowledging that most people searching "NFC business card india" want to buy a physical card — the page must serve both the physical product intent and the smart identity platform angle
- Explain the NFC chip in simple terms: "a passive chip that stores a link — tap your phone against it and a webpage opens, no app needed" — this is still necessary for Indian SMB buyers who are evaluating NFC cards for the first time
- Name the QR backup explicitly: every India-market NFC card should include a QR code on the back, because not every scenario allows a tap (phone cases, crowded events, tech-averse recipients); this is a buying criterion that no other India page currently addresses
- WhatsApp masking must be named — "your WhatsApp number is exposed the moment someone receives your traditional NFC card, unless the platform adds a masking layer"
- Use INR pricing wherever pricing is mentioned — do not reference USD
- Mention that Indian Android users (95% of the smartphone market) can read NFC natively on devices sold after 2018 — this removes the "does it work on Indian phones?" objection directly in the copy
- Avoid calling ProfileTap an "NFC card company" — it is a smart identity management platform that sells NFC cards as part of a broader identity ecosystem
- The page targets buyers who are likely comparing against TapMo and TapOnn — name both as context ("India-native alternatives include TapMo and TapOnn; the key differentiator is...") rather than pretending the competitive set doesn't exist; honest comparison copy builds trust
